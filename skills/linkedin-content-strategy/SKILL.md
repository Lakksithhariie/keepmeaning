# LinkedIn Content Strategy System

Most LinkedIn content fails before a word is written. The failure is 
strategic — not stylistic. Writers default to whatever feels relevant 
today, post inconsistently, and burn out because they have no system 
for knowing what to say next.

This skill provides a complete strategic operating system for LinkedIn 
content. It answers three questions before any post is drafted:

1. **Who is this post for, and what do I want them to do?**
2. **Which part of my expertise does this draw from?**
3. **Does this advance my strategic presence — or just fill a slot?**

Without answers to these three questions, post quality is random. 
With them, every post has a purpose, a place, and a reader.

## Part 1 — The Audience Funnel Model (TOFU / MOFU / BOFU)

LinkedIn is a discovery platform, a credibility platform, and a 
conversion platform — simultaneously. Most creators treat it as only 
one of the three and wonder why their content doesn't produce results.

The TOFU/MOFU/BOFU model maps content type to audience relationship stage.
Every content calendar needs all three tiers, in the right ratio.

### 1.1 — TOFU (Top of Funnel) — Discovery Content

**Audience:** People who don't know you yet. Scrolling past.
**Goal:** Get followed. Build recognition. Earn the "see more" click.
**Relationship stage:** Stranger → Aware

**What TOFU content does:**
- Speaks to a broad problem inside your niche
- Has the highest share and comment potential
- Introduces your perspective without requiring prior knowledge of you
- Gets distributed beyond your existing audience via hashtags, 
  shares, and algorithm amplification

**TOFU content types:**
- Strong opinion on an industry trend (Hard Claim hook)
- Counterintuitive observation most people in your space would recognize
- Relatable tension your ideal reader has definitely experienced
- Surprising statistic or result with wide applicability
- A short story that illustrates a universal challenge in your domain

**TOFU rules:**
- Never assume the reader knows who you are
- The value must exist entirely within the post — no "DM me to learn more"
- The hook must work for someone who has never seen your name before
- Avoid insider language that only existing followers understand
- TOFU posts should account for ~40% of your calendar

**TOFU post example framework:**
Hook: A problem or observation anyone in [industry] would recognize
Body: Your specific take on why it happens (the mechanism)
End: An implication or question that invites a stranger to respond

---

### 1.2 — MOFU (Middle of Funnel) — Credibility Content

**Audience:** People who follow you or have seen you before. 
They're watching but haven't decided if you're worth their full attention.
**Goal:** Build trust. Establish authority. Deepen the relationship.
**Relationship stage:** Aware → Interested → Trusting

**What MOFU content does:**
- Demonstrates depth, not just breadth
- Shows your thinking process, not just conclusions
- Builds a coherent intellectual identity over time
- Gives followers a reason to come back

**MOFU content types:**
- Behind-the-scenes of how you make decisions
- A breakdown of something you've actually built or shipped
- A contrarian framework (not just an opinion — a structured way of thinking)
- Lessons from a specific project, client, or period (with real numbers)
- Your process for solving a problem your audience faces
- A mistake you made and the specific reason it happened
- A comparison of two approaches with your clear verdict

**MOFU rules:**
- Assumes some familiarity with your work and domain
- Can reference previous posts, ongoing projects, or your company
- Depth is the primary value — go further than most people would
- Specificity is non-negotiable — generic frameworks are TOFU, 
  not MOFU
- MOFU posts should account for ~40% of your calendar

**MOFU post example framework:**
Hook: A specific result, decision, or realization from your work
Body: The full context — what you did, what happened, why it matters
End: The honest implication, including what you still don't know

---

### 1.3 — BOFU (Bottom of Funnel) — Conversion Content

**Audience:** People who already trust you. Have read your posts, 
maybe replied, maybe DM'd. They know what you do.
**Goal:** Drive action — a reply, a DM, a click, a booking, a referral.
**Relationship stage:** Trusting → Acting

**What BOFU content does:**
- Makes a clear, specific offer or invitation
- Doesn't apologize for being direct
- Converts the trust built by TOFU and MOFU posts into tangible outcomes
- Builds the business case for what you do without being salesy

**BOFU content types:**
- A specific case study with a named outcome (anonymized if needed)
- A direct invitation to work together, join a waitlist, or book a call
- A "who this is for" post that describes your ideal client/reader exactly
- Social proof post — a result a client or user got, told as a story
- A post that describes your service by describing the problem it solves
- An announcement with genuine context (not just "I'm excited to share...")

**BOFU rules:**
- Be explicit about the action you want — vague BOFU posts get 
  appreciation but not action
- Never let BOFU posts dominate the calendar — they erode trust 
  if overused
- The best BOFU posts don't feel like BOFU — they feel like MOFU 
  posts that happen to end with a clear next step
- BOFU posts should account for ~20% of your calendar
- NEVER write BOFU to cold audiences — only post these when your 
  TOFU/MOFU base has been established

**BOFU post example framework:**
Hook: A specific result or problem (theirs or a client's)
Body: The context that makes the result meaningful
End: A clear, specific, non-apologetic next step

### 1.4 — The Ideal Content Ratio

For a healthy LinkedIn presence across any 4-week period:

CONTENT_RATIO = {
"TOFU": "40%", # ~2 posts/week if posting 5x/week
"MOFU": "40%", # ~2 posts/week
"BOFU": "20%" # ~1 post/week
}

**For early-stage accounts (under 1,000 followers):**
Shift to 60% TOFU / 30% MOFU / 10% BOFU until the follower base 
has sufficient depth for MOFU to land.

**For established accounts (5,000+ followers):**
You can shift to 30% TOFU / 50% MOFU / 20% BOFU — your existing 
audience is large enough that MOFU posts also function as discovery.

## Part 2 — Content Pillar Design

Content pillars are the 3–5 recurring themes that define what you 
write about. They exist to create coherence across your LinkedIn 
presence — so that a new visitor can look at 10 posts and understand 
exactly who you are and what you're building.

### 2.1 — The Pillar Design Formula

A strong content pillar sits at the intersection of three forces:

PILLAR_VALIDITY = {
"expertise": "You have direct, specific experience here",
"audience_need":"Your target reader actively struggles with or cares about this",
"differentiation":"Your take is genuinely different from what's already said"
}

A pillar that satisfies only expertise = diary writing. Nobody cares.
A pillar that satisfies only audience need = generalist content. Forgettable.
A pillar that satisfies only differentiation = contrarian noise. No authority.

All three = a content pillar worth building.

### 2.2 — The 4 Pillar Archetypes

**Pillar Type 1 — The Domain Pillar**
The core subject area of your professional expertise. 
This is what you are known for.

Examples:
- "B2B sales strategy" for a sales consultant
- "Tax automation for Indian startups" for a fintech founder
- "Product design for non-designers" for a UX educator

Rules: This pillar must never become generic. Every post in this pillar 
should carry a specific insight from your experience, not just advice 
that any expert in the field would give.

---

**Pillar Type 2 — The Process Pillar**
How you work, how you think, how you build. This pillar pulls back 
the curtain on your operating system.

Examples:
- "How I run discovery calls with enterprise clients"
- "My product decision-making framework"
- "How I approach building in public without oversharing"

Rules: Process pillars live or die on specificity. Vague process 
("I always start by defining the problem") is worthless. Specific 
process ("I always start by asking the client what success looks like 
in 90 days — and then watch what they don't say") is gold.

---

**Pillar Type 3 — The Opinion Pillar**
Your contrarian or non-obvious views on your industry, your craft, 
or adjacent areas. This is your intellectual identity pillar.

Examples:
- "Why most SaaS onboarding is designed for the company, not the user"
- "Why LinkedIn reach is a vanity metric for B2B founders"
- "Why 'move fast and break things' is only good advice for people 
   who can afford to break things"

Rules: Opinions must be held, not performed. Only write opinion pillar 
posts about things you've genuinely argued about in real conversations. 
If you haven't defended this view in a meeting, it's not a real opinion.

---

**Pillar Type 4 — The Story Pillar**
The personal and professional narrative of your journey. Failures, 
pivots, decisions, relationships, formative moments.

Examples:
- "The two years I spent building the wrong thing"
- "What I've learned from 40 investor rejections"
- "The team decision I regret most"

Rules: Story pillars require the most courage and produce the most 
connection. The only rule is radical specificity — no vague vulnerability. 
Dates, people (anonymized is fine), numbers, and specific quotes.

### 2.3 — Pillar-to-Funnel Mapping

PILLAR_FUNNEL_MAP = {
"Domain Pillar": ["TOFU", "MOFU"], # Broad expertise → deep expertise
"Process Pillar": ["MOFU"], # Assumes some familiarity
"Opinion Pillar": ["TOFU", "MOFU"], # Opinions attract AND deepen
"Story Pillar": ["MOFU", "BOFU"] # Trust-building → conversion
}

## Part 3 — Post Idea Generation System

The most common content block isn't a lack of ideas — it's a lack 
of a system for surfacing ideas from experience that's already there.

### 3.1 — The Experience Mining Protocol

Run this protocol to generate 20+ post ideas from any professional's 
experience in under 30 minutes.

**Mine Category 1 — Decisions**
List 5 significant decisions made in the last 12 months. For each:
- What were the options?
- What made it hard?
- What did you choose and why?
- What happened?
- What would you do differently?

Each question is a potential post. A decision with 5 answers = 5 posts.

**Mine Category 2 — Mistakes**
List 3 things you got wrong in the last 18 months. For each:
- What did you believe before?
- When did you realize you were wrong?
- What was the specific trigger or evidence?
- What changed after you corrected it?
- Who else might be making this same mistake right now?

Each mistake = 2-3 posts minimum.

**Mine Category 3 — Conversations**
List 5 conversations (client, team, investor, peer) that changed 
how you think about something. For each:
- What was the question or challenge that started it?
- What did the other person say that surprised you?
- How did your thinking shift?
- What would you tell yourself before that conversation?

Each conversation = 1-2 posts.

**Mine Category 4 — Patterns Observed**
List 5 things you've noticed happening repeatedly in your field, 
with your clients, or in your market. For each:
- How many instances have you actually observed?
- Why do you think this pattern exists?
- What does it mean for someone in your audience's position?
- What's the non-obvious implication?

Each pattern = 1 high-value TOFU post.

**Mine Category 5 — Things You Believe That Others Dispute**
List 3 views you hold that colleagues or peers push back on. For each:
- What's your exact position?
- What's the strongest argument against it?
- What's the evidence or experience that makes you hold it anyway?
- Who specifically would benefit from hearing this view?

Each belief = 1 strong Opinion Pillar post.

### 3.2 — The Repurposing Engine

Post ideas don't have to be new. Your best thinking already exists 
somewhere — it just needs to be extracted and reshaped.

**Source 1 — Client/User Conversations**
Every discovery call, sales call, support ticket, and user interview 
contains post material. Specifically:
- The question a client asked that you didn't expect
- The assumption a prospect had that was completely wrong
- The result a client shared that surprised even you
- The objection you heard for the 10th time (and finally have a 
  complete answer to)

**Source 2 — Internal Documents**
Every memo, PRD, post-mortem, proposal, or strategy doc you've 
written contains posts. Look for:
- The section you struggled most to write (it's the real insight)
- The recommendation you almost didn't include (it's the bold take)
- The data table that surprised you when you built it
- The decision log entry where two options were genuinely close

**Source 3 — Emails and Slack Messages**
Your most natural, human writing exists in your async communication. 
Look for:
- Explanations you wrote to a team member that took real effort
- Responses to hard questions from investors, clients, or partners
- Threads where your thinking evolved visibly over multiple messages
- Advice you gave that the other person said was unusually useful

**Source 4 — Previous Posts That Underperformed**
A post that got low engagement isn't necessarily a bad idea. 
It may have had a weak hook, wrong timing, or wrong structure. 
Before discarding an idea, ask:
- Did the hook earn the click?
- Did the body deliver what the hook promised?
- Was it the right funnel tier for the audience size at that time?
- Has anything changed (new data, new example, stronger opinion) 
  that would make a rewrite stronger?

---

### 3.3 — The Idea Quality Filter

Before adding any idea to the content calendar, run it through 
this filter. Ideas that fail the filter get either refined or cut.

IDEA_QUALITY_FILTER = {
"specificity_test": "Can I write this with at least 2 specific details
that only I would know?",
"audience_test": "Is there a real person whose next action is
affected by reading this?",
"differentiation_test": "Would 10 other people in my field write this
the exact same way?",
"courage_test": "Am I slightly uncomfortable about how direct
this is?"
}

A post that passes all 4 = publish.
A post that fails specificity = add concrete detail before scheduling.
A post that fails differentiation = find the non-obvious angle or cut.
A post that fails courage = ask "what am I not saying that I actually 
believe?" and add it.

## Part 4 — The 30-Day Content Planning System

### 4.1 — The Planning Session Protocol

Run this session once per month (60 minutes maximum) to generate 
a full 30-day content calendar.

**Step 1 — Mine (20 minutes)**
Run the Experience Mining Protocol from Part 3.1. 
Generate raw ideas without filtering. Target: 25–35 raw ideas.

**Step 2 — Filter (10 minutes)**
Run each raw idea through the Idea Quality Filter from Part 3.3. 
Target: 15–20 ideas that pass. Cut the rest without guilt.

**Step 3 — Assign Funnel Tier (5 minutes)**
For each remaining idea, assign a TOFU/MOFU/BOFU tier. 
Check against the ideal content ratio in Part 1.4. 
Rebalance if needed.

**Step 4 — Assign Post Type (5 minutes)**
For each idea, assign a post type from the POST_TYPE_MAP in 
linkedin-post-structure, Part 7. 
Ensure variety — don't schedule 5 list posts in a row.

**Step 5 — Sequence the Calendar (10 minutes)**
Place posts into the calendar. Sequencing rules:
- Never open a week with a BOFU post
- Alternate high-effort (story, case study) with low-effort 
  (observation, opinion) posts
- If you have a BOFU post, precede it with 2+ MOFU posts 
  that build context for the offer
- Schedule your highest-confidence posts for Tuesday-Thursday 
  (peak LinkedIn engagement days)

**Step 6 — Write 3 Posts Immediately (10 minutes)**
Before ending the planning session, write 3 full hooks 
(first lines only) for your top 3 posts. This prevents 
calendar items from staying theoretical.

## Part 5 — Posting Cadence and Frequency Logic

### 5.1 — Frequency by Stage

**Early stage (0–500 followers): Post 3–4x per week**
At this stage, the goal is building a content library and finding 
your highest-performing post types. Volume matters more than 
perfection because you're gathering signal.

**Growth stage (500–5,000 followers): Post 4–5x per week**
Consistency is the primary driver of algorithmic amplification 
at this stage. Missing 2+ days in a row resets momentum.

**Established (5,000+ followers): Post 3–4x per week**
Quality over quantity becomes the right trade-off. Your existing 
followers amplify your best work. One exceptional post beats 
three average ones.

**Do NOT post:**
- More than once per day (algorithm penalty, audience fatigue)
- Fewer than 3x per week for more than 2 consecutive weeks 
  (follower drop-off and algorithm suppression)

### 5.2 — Time-of-Day Logic

Optimal posting windows (IST, for India-based audiences):

POSTING_WINDOWS = {
"primary": "07:30–09:00", # Pre-work scroll, highest early engagement
"secondary": "12:00–13:30", # Lunch break scroll
"tertiary": "18:00–19:30", # Post-work scroll
"avoid": ["22:00–06:00", # Low traffic
"Saturday afternoon", # Low professional traffic
"Sunday morning"] # Competes with personal content
}

For global/US-facing audiences: 
- Shift to 8:00–10:00 EST or 17:00–19:00 EST equivalents

### 5.3 — Consistency Over Virality

The single most destructive content strategy pattern on LinkedIn:

Post 5x when motivated → Go viral once → Expect repeat virality →
Disappointed when it doesn't repeat → Post nothing for 2 weeks →
Audience forgets → Start over

A post that gets 200 impressions every day for 90 days compounds 
into 18,000 impressions. A post that goes viral once and gets 50,000 
impressions then disappears = one month of visibility with no compounding.

Consistency is the only non-negotiable rule of LinkedIn strategy. 
No exception.

## Part 6 — Topic-to-Pillar Mapping for the Agent

When the agent receives a raw topic or idea, use this mapping 
logic to classify it before generating:

TOPIC_CLASSIFICATION = {
"keywords": {
"how I built / how we shipped / what we learned": "Process Pillar, MOFU",
"most people are wrong about / the real reason": "Opinion Pillar, TOFU",
"X results in Y days / here's what happened": "Domain Pillar, TOFU/MOFU",
"I made a mistake / I was wrong / I regret": "Story Pillar, MOFU",
"if you do [X], here's what to expect": "Domain Pillar, TOFU",
"working with clients taught me / after N calls": "Process Pillar, MOFU",
"we're opening spots / looking for / if this fits":"Story Pillar, BOFU",
"the problem with [industry] is": "Opinion Pillar, TOFU"
}
}

If the topic doesn't match a known pattern, ask the author:
1. Is this about what you know, how you work, or what you believe?
2. Is the audience stranger, follower, or ready-to-act?

The answers map directly to pillar type and funnel tier.

## Part 7 — Strategy Anti-Patterns to Avoid

**Anti-Pattern 1 — Motivation Posting**
Writing about hard work, consistency, mindset, and persistence 
without anchoring it to specific results. This is the lowest-value 
content category on LinkedIn and the most over-produced.
Rule: Every motivational observation must be attached to a specific 
event from your life or it doesn't get written.

**Anti-Pattern 2 — Trend-Chasing**
Writing about a trending topic that has nothing to do with your 
content pillars because it's getting engagement. Short-term reach, 
long-term incoherence. Your audience follows you for your pillars. 
You can reference a trend — you cannot become it.

**Anti-Pattern 3 — Engagement Bait Without Depth**
Posts designed purely to get comments ("Drop a ✅ if you agree", 
"Comment YES if you want the template") with no actual intellectual 
value. These inflate vanity metrics and attract the wrong audience.

**Anti-Pattern 4 — The Never-BOFU Trap**
Being so afraid of seeming salesy that BOFU posts never get written. 
Your audience wants to work with you — they followed you because 
of your TOFU and MOFU posts. Not telling them how to work with 
you is a disservice, not humility.

**Anti-Pattern 5 — Strategy-Without-Calendar**
Having clear pillars and a TOFU/MOFU/BOFU plan but no actual 
calendar. Ideas that aren't scheduled don't get written. 
Without a calendar, the strategy is theoretical. 
Fill the calendar before closing the planning session.

## Part 8 — Agent Execution Instructions

When generating content strategy output, follow this sequence:

STEP 1: Identify or confirm the 3–5 content pillars for this author
STEP 2: Classify the incoming topic against TOPIC_CLASSIFICATION
STEP 3: Assign funnel tier (TOFU / MOFU / BOFU) with rationale
STEP 4: Check the content ratio — is this tier under/over-represented
in recent posts? Adjust if needed.
STEP 5: Select post type from POST_TYPE_MAP (in linkedin-post-structure)
STEP 6: Generate the post using linkedin-voice and linkedin-post-structure
STEP 7: Tag the completed post with its pillar and funnel tier for
calendar tracking

- Run the 30-Day Calendar Template from Part 4.2 as a starting structure
- Populate each slot with a specific topic from the author's 
  experience mining
- Deliver calendar as a table with: Day | Funnel | Pillar | Post Type | Hook Draft
